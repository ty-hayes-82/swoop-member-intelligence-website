## Narrative Verdict

This is one of the sharpest demo/contact pages I've seen in niche B2B SaaS. It does something most demo pages fail at entirely: it reframes the call itself as a deliverable, not a sales pitch. The headline "Get a Custom Retention Plan. Not a Pitch Deck." immediately neutralizes the buyer's #1 objection — that they'll waste 30 minutes being sold to. The page earns trust through specificity (30 minutes, your data, mutual NDA, deleted in 30 days) and closes with a testimonial that names a dollar figure. The single most impactful improvement would be adding a second proof point — one testimonial from one GM at one club type leaves a narrow credibility footprint for a page asking someone to hand over their tee-sheet data.

## The Rewrite List

### 1. Second-section headline

**Current:** "See what your club misses today and can recover tomorrow."

**Why it underperforms:** This is good but slightly generic — "misses today / recover tomorrow" is a formulaic before/after construction that doesn't carry the same punch as the primary headline. It also doesn't tell the reader anything new; the top section already established that Swoop finds what you're missing.

**Suggested rewrite:** "Your tee sheet is talking. Here's how we listen." — This ties directly to the data-connection mechanic described in the subtext, creates intrigue, and differentiates from the first section's promise-oriented framing by shifting to method.

### 2. CTA button text

**Current:** "Get My Custom Retention Plan"

**Why it's nearly perfect but could be sharper:** This is already strong — it's specific and outcome-oriented. But "My" feels slightly consumer-y for a B2B GM audience. The word "Custom" is doing redundant work since the entire page already establishes customization.

**Suggested rewrite:** "Get My Club's Retention Plan" — Swapping "Custom" for "My Club's" makes it feel like the deliverable is already being built for their specific property. Small change, meaningful shift in ownership feeling.

### 3. Subtext under second headline

**Current:** "Book a live walkthrough with your own operating scenarios: tee sheet leakage, at-risk members, F&B staffing pressure, and revenue pipeline blind spots."

**Why it partially fails:** "Revenue pipeline blind spots" is abstract consultant-speak. Every other item in the list is concrete and visualizable — a GM knows exactly what "tee sheet leakage" and "F&B staffing pressure" look like. "Revenue pipeline blind spots" is vague by comparison. It also breaks the parallel structure.

**Suggested rewrite:** "Book a live walkthrough with your own operating scenarios: tee sheet leakage, at-risk members, F&B staffing pressure, and missed event and ancillary revenue."

### 4. The data-deletion promise

**Current:** "Your data under mutual NDA. We never share club data across engagements. Deleted within 30 days if you don't move forward."

**Why it's good but buries the lead:** The deletion guarantee is the most trust-building detail on the page, but it's the last bullet in a checklist, formatted identically to less consequential items. For a GM who's nervous about handing over member data to an unknown vendor, this should hit harder.

**Suggested rewrite:** Keep the text nearly identical but break it into its own callout block beneath the checklist:

> **Your data stays yours.** Mutual NDA on every engagement. We never share data across clubs. If you don't move forward, everything is deleted within 30 days.

### 5. The "What happens next" step 2

**Current:** "We pull a sample brief from your tee sheet and POS (5-min secure read) — Seamless read-only API connection to Jonas, Clubessential, Northstar, etc. Zero IT required."

**Why it partially fails:** "Seamless" is a cliché flag — it's doing no work here. "5-min secure read" is excellent specificity that earns trust, but "seamless read-only API connection" is the kind of technical language that might sail over a GM's head or, worse, make them think they need to involve their IT person (the opposite of what "Zero IT required" is trying to communicate). The sentence contradicts itself internally — technical jargon followed by "Zero IT."

**Suggested rewrite:**
"2. We pull a sample brief from your tee sheet and POS (5-min secure read).
We connect directly to Jonas, Clubessential, Northstar, and others — read-only, no IT involvement, nothing to install."

## The Missing Story

**What's absent: A second testimonial from a different club archetype.**

Robert Torres at Meridian Hills CC (340-member equity private club) is a strong proof point. But a GM at a 1,200-member semi-private club, or a COO at a resort-style property, reads that quote and thinks: *"That's a small equity club — does this work for us?"*

The page needs one more voice — ideally from a different club size, type, or geography — to widen the credibility aperture. It doesn't need to be a paragraph. Even a single line:

> *"We found $47K in lapsed-member revenue we'd written off."*
> — Jennifer Walsh, COO · Lakewood Country Club · 1,100-member semi-private

Two quotes from two club types transforms "this worked for one club" into "this works for clubs like mine." On a page asking someone to hand over their operational data, that second proof point is the difference between "interesting" and "I'll fill out the form."

A secondary missing element: **a visual or textual preview of what the "prioritized action list" deliverable actually looks like.** The page promises you'll leave with a ranked list of revenue and retention gaps — but showing even a blurred or anonymized thumbnail of that output would make the value tangible. Right now it's a promise. A preview makes it a near-guarantee.
