## The Rewrite List

### 1. Hero Subheadline

**Current text:** *"Most club software forces you to become an analyst. Swoop watches your data so nothing is missed—every morning, it briefs you on what changed, what's at risk, and what to act on. No dashboards, no reports, no digging through data. Just a daily intelligence briefing."*

**Why it fails:** This is four sentences doing the work of one. "Forces you to become an analyst" is decent but generic. "No dashboards, no reports, no digging through data" is a list of negatives without a vivid positive. The phrase "Just a daily intelligence briefing" repeats what the headline already said. The whole block reads like a first draft — all the ideas are here but they haven't been compressed.

**Suggested rewrite:**
*"Your POS, CRM, and tee sheet generate thousands of data points a week. Nobody has time to read them. Swoop does — and every morning, it tells your team exactly who needs attention, what changed, and what to do next."*

## The Missing Story

**What's absent: A "Day in the Life" before/after narrative.**

This page has all the structural ingredients — metrics, testimonials, team bios, FAQ — but it's missing the single most persuasive element for its audience: a vivid, concrete depiction of what a GM's morning looks like *with* Swoop vs. *without* it.

Imagine a split-screen or sequential narrative:

> **Without Swoop:** You walk in at 7:15. There's a stack of reports from three systems. Your F&B director mentions a member complained last week — you didn't know. You pull up the CRM, cross-reference the tee sheet, check dining logs. By 9 AM, you've spent 90 minutes becoming an analyst instead of a leader.

> **With Swoop:** You open one briefing at 7:15. It tells you: 3 members haven't visited in 45+ days (one is a board member's spouse). F&B covers per round dropped 11% this month. And a new member just hit their 5th visit — perfect time for a personal check-in. By 7:30, you've already made two calls and flagged one issue for your team.

This kind of scenario does something no metric or testimonial can: it lets the GM *see themselves* in the product. It converts abstract intelligence into felt experience. The testimonials from founding-partner GMs gesture toward this ("The founding belief is the first slide made complete sense to every single person I've shown it to") but they're quoted fragments, not narratives.

The page also entirely lacks a **founder origin story**. Why did Tyler, Jordan, and Alex build this? What club experience or observation sparked it? GMs are relationship people — they want to know they're working with humans who *get* the club world, not just technologists who found a market. A two-paragraph founder story ("We spent a year sitting in club offices watching GMs manually piece together member data from five systems...") would do more for trust than any feature list.
