## The Conversion Audit

### Page: Contact / Demo (this is the only page under review)

**Page Job:** Get a qualified club GM or COO to submit their name, club, and email to book a 30-minute live walkthrough using their own data.

**Is it doing the job?** Mostly yes — for visitors who scroll. The bottom half of the page is genuinely well-constructed conversion architecture. But the top half is a content island with no conversion mechanism, and the two halves feel like separate pages stitched together.

---

### Section-by-Section Breakdown:

#### 1. HERO SECTION (First Viewport)

**What's working:**
- **"Get a Custom Retention Plan. Not a Pitch Deck."** — This is an A+ headline. It's specific, outcome-oriented, and uses contrast framing (Cialdini's "rejection-then-retreat" adjacent — you're reframing what a demo IS). The "Not a Pitch Deck" line directly handles the #1 objection to booking any SaaS demo: "I don't want to sit through a sales presentation."
- The subhead is strong: "In 30 minutes, we load your tee-sheet data into Swoop and show you exactly where revenue is leaking and which members are quietly disengaging." This is concrete, time-bounded, and uses the prospect's own data as the hook (endowment effect — it's about THEIR club, not your product).
- **"WHAT YOU'LL LEAVE WITH"** checklist is excellent. Four specific deliverables — ranked revenue gaps, benchmarks against 7 founding-partner clubs, a 90-day action plan, and mutual NDA protection. This transforms the CTA from "book a demo" into "get a free consulting deliverable" (reciprocity principle).
- The NDA and data deletion language ("Deleted within 30 days if you don't move forward") is proactive objection handling for a data-sensitive buyer persona. Club GMs worry about member data. This is smart.

**What's broken:**
- **There is NO CTA button, no form, no anchor link, and no directional cue in the entire hero section.** A visitor who lands here and doesn't scroll sees a compelling pitch with literally zero way to act on it. This violates the most fundamental conversion principle: every viewport needs a clear next action.
- No visual arrow, no "Book your walkthrough below" text, no sticky header CTA, no scroll indicator. The hero section is a dead end.
- The audience is never explicitly named. "Your club" is implied, but a first-time visitor from a Google ad doesn't see "For private club General Managers and COOs" anywhere. This matters for qualification and for the visitor's self-identification ("Is this for me?").

**#1 Fix:** Add a prominent CTA button in the hero section — either a "Get My Custom Retention Plan" button that anchor-links to the form below, or duplicate the form inline. At minimum, add a directional cue: "↓ Book your 30-minute walkthrough" with a scroll anchor.

#### 2. CONVERSION SECTION (Second Viewport — Dark Background)

**What's working:**
- **"See what your club misses today and can recover tomorrow."** — Good secondary headline. Loss aversion framing ("misses" and "recover") is textbook Kahneman. The temporal contrast (today/tomorrow) creates urgency without being manipulative.
- **The form is beautifully minimal: 3 fields — Name, Club, Email.** No phone number, no company size, no "how did you hear about us." This is a low-friction form that respects the buyer's time. The placeholder text "e.g., Pine Valley Golf Club" is a smart specificity cue that also subtly signals "we work with prestigious clubs" (authority/aspirational anchoring).
- **CTA button copy: "Get My Custom Retention Plan"** — This is outcome-oriented, first-person ("My"), and reframes the demo as a deliverable. Significantly better than "Book a Demo" or "Submit." The orange color contrasts well against the dark background and the white form.
- **Security badges below the form:** "AES-256 Encryption · SOC 2 Type II (Audit Active) · Mutual NDA on Every Engagement." These are real, specific trust signals placed exactly where they belong — directly below the submit button, at the moment of maximum commitment anxiety. Excellent placement (Fogg Behavior Model — reducing anxiety at the trigger point).
- **The testimonial from Robert Torres, GM, Meridian Hills CC, 340-member equity private club** is placed below the form and is highly specific: "Swoop flagged a member we were about to lose. One dinner comp and a follow-up call saved $18K in annual dues." This is a named person, named club, specific club size, specific dollar outcome. This is textbook social proof done right — specific, attributed, outcome-quantified.
- **"What happens next:" numbered sequence** (1. Confirm slot within 1 business day, 2. Pull sample brief from tee sheet and POS — 5-min secure read, 3. 30-min call — you keep the prioritized action list regardless). This is a commitment/consistency play — by showing the clear, low-effort next steps, you reduce the perceived cost of saying yes. The "you keep the prioritized action list regardless" is risk reversal (reciprocity + loss aversion).
- **"No credit card · 30 minutes · Your club's own data"** — Three trust accelerators in one line. Efficient.
- **"Or email us at demo@swoopgolf.com"** — Alternative contact method for people who won't fill out forms. Good friction reduction.
- **Integration callout:** "Seamless read-only API connection to Jonas, Clubessential, Northstar, etc. Zero IT required." This handles a HUGE objection for club GMs who are not technical and who dread involving their IT person or board. "Zero IT required" is potentially the most important line on the page for this buyer persona.

**What's broken:**
- **The testimonial is BELOW the form, not above or beside it.** Social proof should appear *before* or *alongside* the commitment point, not after. A visitor filling out the form has already scrolled past the testimonial's optimal placement. Move it to directly above or to the left of the form.
- **Only ONE testimonial.** For a page asking someone to hand over their club's tee sheet data, one named reference point is thin. Two or three short quotes from different club types (equity, semi-private, resort) would dramatically increase confidence. The "7 founding-partner clubs" referenced in the hero section are never named or shown.
- The "Book a live walkthrough with your own operating scenarios: tee sheet leakage, at-risk members, F&B staffing pressure, and revenue pipeline blind spots" — this supporting copy is good but it's left-aligned text competing with the form on the right, and the visual hierarchy doesn't clearly lead the eye from this text → form. The two columns feel somewhat disconnected.

**#1 Fix:** Move the Robert Torres testimonial to directly above the form (between the "See what your club misses" headline and the form fields), and add at least one more specific testimonial from a different club type.

### Additional Notes:

**What this page does uncommonly well:**
- The reframing of a demo as a *deliverable* ("Custom Retention Plan," "90-day action plan — yours to keep, no strings attached") is elite positioning. It transforms the value exchange from "give us your info so we can sell to you" into "give us your info so we can give you something valuable." This is reciprocity principle at its best and should be protected and amplified.
- The data security handling (NDA, SOC 2, AES-256, 30-day deletion) is perfectly calibrated for the private club market where member data is sacred. This isn't generic trust badging — it's buyer-persona-specific objection handling.
- The 3-field form is the right call. For this market (senior club executives, not self-serve SaaS buyers), asking for Name + Club + Email is the minimum viable qualification. Resist the temptation to add fields.

**What's conspicuously absent:**
- **No pricing signal whatsoever.** Not "Free assessment," not "Plans start at," not "Custom pricing based on club size." Enterprise buyers in this persona often won't fill out a form if they have zero frame of reference on cost. Even a "Free for your first club" or "No cost for the initial assessment" badge near the form would help.
- **No logos or club name badges.** The hero references "7 founding-partner clubs" but never shows them. Even anonymized ("7 private clubs across 5 states") with silhouetted logos would add weight. Named logos would be far better.
- **No video or visual preview** of what the actual walkthrough looks like. A 30-second screen recording or even a static screenshot of "what you'll see on the call" would reduce uncertainty about what they're signing up for (reducing the unknown = reducing friction per the Fogg model).
