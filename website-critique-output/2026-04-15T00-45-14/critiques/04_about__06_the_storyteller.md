## The Rewrite List

### 1. Hero Headline

**Current:** *"Stop digging for answers. Get one actionable morning briefing."*

**Why it fails:** "Stop digging for answers" is a generic pain statement that could apply to any BI, analytics, or reporting tool. "One actionable morning briefing" is better — it's specific — but it leads with format (a briefing) rather than outcome (what the briefing does for the GM's day, decisions, or results). A competitor could swap their name in and this headline would still work.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> **"Built by a GM who was drowning in spreadsheets — so you never have to."**
> *One AI-powered morning briefing with the member intelligence that took your team hours to compile.*

This pulls the founder credibility into the hero where it belongs, creates immediate identification with the target buyer, and still communicates the product format.

---

### 2. Subheadline / Hero Supporting Copy

**Current:** *"Your POS, CRM, and tee sheet hold the answers. Swoop reads them overnight, so every morning you see exactly who's at risk, what changed, and what to act on."*

**Why it fails:** This is solid but buried below a weaker headline. "Reads them overnight" is vague — does it mean batch processing? The phrase "what changed" is too abstract. This copy actually does good work explaining the mechanics, but it needs sharper specificity.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> *"Swoop connects to your POS, CRM, and tee sheet overnight. By 7 AM, you see which members are disengaging, which F&B patterns shifted, and exactly what your team should do about it — no spreadsheet required."*

---

### 3. Section Headline: "An extension of your team, not a disruption to it."

**Current:** *"An extension of your team, not a disruption to it."*

**Why it fails:** This is a defensive framing that sounds like every SaaS tool trying to overcome implementation objections. It's vague — what does "extension of your team" actually mean? The subtext reads as "we know you're scared of new software" rather than "here's what your team looks like with Swoop."

**Suggested rewrite:**
> **"Your team stays the same. Their mornings don't."**
> *Swoop doesn't replace anyone — it gives your membership director, F&B manager, and front desk the intelligence they used to spend hours compiling.*

This is concrete, names the actual roles on the team, and reframes the value as time recaptured.

---

### 4. Section Headline: "Intelligence in action."

**Current:** *"Intelligence in action."*

**Why it fails:** This is pure cliché. It says nothing specific about Swoop, the club industry, or the outcomes a GM cares about. It's the kind of headline you write as a placeholder and forget to replace. Below it: *"Here's how our Founding Partner Clubs are performing with Swoop"* — that supporting line is actually better than the headline itself.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> **"Here's what Founding Partner clubs are seeing."**

Then let the stats (6 days, 91%, $312, $1.38M) do the work. The data is strong — the headline shouldn't compete with it.

### 5. CTA: "Book a walkthrough"

**Current:** *"Book a walkthrough"* (appears in hero and repeated throughout)

**Why it fails:** "Book a walkthrough" is generic SaaS-speak. For a GM who gets pitched by 10 vendors a quarter, this doesn't signal that the experience will be different, tailored, or valuable. It also doesn't reduce friction — a "walkthrough" sounds like a time commitment.

**Suggested rewrite (hero CTA):**
> **"See your club's morning briefing"**

**Suggested rewrite (mid-page CTA near stats):**
> **"See how Founding Partners are using Swoop →"**

These are outcome-oriented and curiosity-driven, not process-oriented.

## The Missing Story

### What's absent: **A Day-in-the-Life scenario**

The founder story establishes "I was the GM." The stats establish "it works." But there's a gaping hole between them: **what does a GM's morning actually look like with Swoop?**

Imagine a section:

> **"It's 7:02 AM. You haven't opened a spreadsheet."**
> *Your morning briefing shows that the Hendersons — 12-year members — haven't booked a tee time in 6 weeks. Their F&B spend dropped 40% last month. Swoop flagged them yesterday. Your membership director already sent a personal check-in. That's the kind of save that used to slip through the cracks until renewal season.*

This would be the emotional bridge between "here's our story" and "here are our stats." It lets the GM see themselves in the product. It makes the abstract ("member intelligence") viscerally concrete. And it would be the most memorable section on the page — because stories about specific people (even fictional composites) are always more memorable than statistics.

**Secondary missing element: named member quotes with context.** The "From founding-partner GMs" testimonials are visible but appear as text blocks with star ratings. If these included the GM's name, club name, and a one-line context (club size, tenure, specific problem they had), they'd carry 3x the credibility. Right now the quote *"I've been running this club for 8 years and nothing has ever given me this kind of visibility"* (if that's representative) needs an identity attached to it. Anonymous social proof in B2B is barely proof at all — a GM putting their name on a recommendation is putting their reputation on the line, which is exactly what the next buyer needs to see.
