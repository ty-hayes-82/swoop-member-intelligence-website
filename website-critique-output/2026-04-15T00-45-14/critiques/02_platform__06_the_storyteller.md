### 2. Section Header: "Six jobs Swoop does before you finish your morning coffee."

**Current:** The six cards appear to describe tasks like flagging at-risk members, scanning activity, pulling reports, etc. — each leading with the *process*.

**Why it fails:** The "morning coffee" framing is charming and effective for the header, but the individual cards squander the setup by listing tasks rather than outcomes. A GM doesn't care that Swoop "scans activity" — they care that they *walk into the Monday board meeting knowing exactly which 12 members need a call this week.*

**Suggested rewrite for each card pattern:**
- **Current pattern:** "Flags members with declining engagement"
- **Rewrite pattern:** "3 members flagged as resignation risks this week — with the exact outreach your team should make today"

The card titles should be outcomes ("$34K in at-risk dues identified overnight") not tasks ("Scans dining and golf activity").

### 3. "Six AI agents working your club — live."

**Current:** "Six AI agents working your club — live."

**Why it fails:** "AI agents" is 2024's most overused SaaS term. Every platform claims agents now. The word "live" adds nothing — what would the alternative be, dead? This header wastes the strongest section of the page (the agent dashboard mockup showing $6,488 appears genuinely impressive).

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Six specialists monitoring your club around the clock — no salaries, no sick days, no missed signals."

This reframes agents as *staff equivalents* (which is how a GM thinks about headcount) and introduces a concrete comparison that justifies the price.

### 4. CTA Buttons: "Request a Demo" (repeated multiple times)

**Current:** "Request a Demo" appears at least 3-4 times throughout the page.

**Why it fails:** It's the most generic CTA in B2B SaaS. It puts the burden on the prospect (you must *request*, then *wait*, then *sit through* a demo). For a GM who's already skeptical of tech vendors, this feels like signing up for a sales pitch, not getting value.

**Suggested rewrites (vary by section context):**
- Hero CTA: **"See What Swoop Finds in Your Club Data"**
- After "morning coffee" section: **"Get Your Club's Risk Score — Free"**
- After agents section: **"Watch the 3-Minute Walkthrough"**
- Final CTA: **"Start Protecting Your Dues This Month"**

Each CTA should promise a *specific next experience*, not a generic process step.

### 5. "Your tools store the data. Swoop decides what to do with it."

**Current:** "Your tools store the data. Swoop decides what to do with it."

**Why it fails:** This is *almost* great. The contrast is sharp. But "decides what to do with it" is vague — it sounds like Swoop is making autonomous decisions, which might actually alarm a GM who wants to stay in control. The section below mentions "Typical launch: 10 business days" and lists integrations, which is strong, but the header oversells autonomy.

**Suggested rewrite:**

This keeps the punchy contrast but repositions Swoop as the *intelligence layer* that empowers the team, not replaces their judgment. The "Monday morning" detail anchors it in the GM's real workflow.

## The Missing Story

**What's absent: A named club's before/after transformation.**

This page does almost everything right architecturally, but it's missing the one element that would convert skeptical GMs from "interesting" to "I need this": **a real story.** Not a logo bar. Not a testimonial snippet. A *narrative.*

Something like:

> *"When Pinetree Country Club's GM Sarah Chen opened Swoop on a Tuesday morning, she saw something her team had missed for months: 23 members hadn't booked a tee time or dining reservation in over 60 days. Twelve of them were in their first two years of membership — the highest-risk cohort for resignation. Her team made personal calls to each one that week. By quarter's end, Pinetree had retained 19 of the 23 — protecting $147K in annual dues. 'Before Swoop, I would have found out about those members when the resignation letters hit my desk,' Chen said."*

This story would:
1. Give the GM a **proxy** — someone like them who made the decision and looked smart for it
2. Put a **dollar figure** on the outcome ($147K in protected dues)
3. Show the **before/after** ("resignation letters on my desk" → "personal calls that week")
4. Make the platform feel **real** — not theoretical, not a mockup, but a Tuesday morning at a club like theirs

Without this, the page is a beautifully structured *promise*. With it, it becomes *proof*. And in B2B, proof is what makes a buyer put their reputation on the line to recommend a new vendor to their board.

**Secondary missing element:** A "Why now?" section. What's changing in the private club industry that makes this urgent? Rising resignation rates? Demographic shifts? A brief "The clubs that figure this out in 2024 will keep their members. The ones that don't will keep losing them to clubs that did." framing would add urgency without being manipulative.
