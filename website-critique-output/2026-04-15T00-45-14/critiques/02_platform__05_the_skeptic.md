## Trust Builders (What's Working)

**1. Domain expertise is clearly demonstrated in the copy.**
The headline "Stop guessing who's drifting. Start protecting your dues." speaks directly to a GM's anxiety. The phrase "drifting" is insider language — it signals someone who understands member retention in the club context. This is not generic SaaS copy.

**2. "Six jobs Swoop does before you finish your morning coffee" — specific, operational framing.**
This section names concrete tasks (e.g., flagging members who haven't booked tee times, identifying spend anomalies, detecting reservation no-shows). These feel like real operational pain points, not marketing abstractions. The framing of six discrete "jobs" gives the product a tangible shape.

**3. "The daily brief, written overnight" — a clear, understandable value proposition.**
Showing what appears to be an AI-generated morning brief is a smart way to concretize the product. A GM can immediately imagine opening this instead of pulling four reports.

**4. "Six AI agents working your club — live" — productizes the AI layer.**
Rather than saying "we use AI," the page names distinct agent functions. This gives the buyer a mental model of what they're buying. The $6,488 figure visible in the UI mockup suggests revenue-impact tracking.

**5. "Your tools store the data. Swoop decides what to do with it" — integration positioning is smart.**
The copy acknowledges that clubs already have POS, CRM, tee sheet, and billing systems. Positioning Swoop as an intelligence layer on top, rather than a replacement, reduces perceived switching cost. The "Typical launch: 14 business days" claim is specific and testable.

**6. "One page replaces four logins" — addresses a real operational frustration.**
This resonates with any GM juggling Northstar/Jonas, ForeTees, CRM, and accounting software.

**7. Data security section is present.**
"Your members' data is safe. Full stop." with mentions of encryption and what appear to be compliance badges shows awareness that clubs handle sensitive member financial data. This is the right instinct, even if the execution is thin.

## The Due Diligence Gaps

A careful buyer would immediately Google/research the following after visiting this page:

1. **"Swoop club intelligence reviews"** — looking for any independent validation. They will likely find nothing, which confirms the "too new to trust" concern.

2. **"Swoop club intelligence founder LinkedIn"** — trying to identify who's behind this. Without a name on the site, this search may fail entirely.

3. **"Swoop club intelligence pricing"** — trying to get any signal on cost before committing to a sales call.

4. **Asking their CMAA peer network** — "Has anyone heard of Swoop? Anyone using it?" In club culture, peer referral is the #1 trust mechanism. The absence of any reference to CMAA, NCA, or club association involvement is a gap.

5. **"Swoop integrations Jonas Northstar Club Essentials ForeTees"** — the page claims to integrate with existing club systems but doesn't name specific platforms. A GM running Jonas wants to know: Does this actually connect to Jonas? Via API? File export? Manual upload?

6. **Data security and compliance documentation** — "Where's the DPA? Where's the SOC 2 report? Who's your hosting provider? Where does member data reside?"

7. **Contract terms and implementation risk** — "What happens if this doesn't work? What's the commitment? Is there an out clause? Who handles onboarding?"

8. **Competitive landscape** — "How does this compare to Cobalt Software, MembersFirst analytics, or just building dashboards in Tableau/Power BI?"
