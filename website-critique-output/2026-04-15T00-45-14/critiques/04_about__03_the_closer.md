## The Conversion Audit

### 1. Hero Section

**Page Job:** Hook a cold GM visitor with a pain point they recognize and get them to either scroll (micro-conversion) or book a walkthrough (macro-conversion).

**Is it doing the job?** Partially. The headline "Stop digging for answers. Get one actionable morning briefing." correctly identifies the pain (manual data digging) but the solution framing ("morning briefing") sounds like a Substack newsletter, not a $1.38M-recovering AI platform. The subhead adds context ("Your POS, CRM, and tee sheet data...") but it's small gray text that many visitors will skim past.

The orange "Book a walkthrough" CTA button is well-positioned and uses decent action language, but "walkthrough" is a mid-commitment word — it implies a 30+ minute demo. For a cold About page visitor, this may be premature.

**#1 Fix:** Rewrite the headline to include a specific, quantified outcome. Replace "Get one actionable morning briefing" with something like: **"Get a daily briefing that tells you which members to call, which tables are empty, and which $312 opportunities you're missing — before 9am."** This marries the mechanism (daily briefing) with the metrics you already have lower on the page, pulling authority proof UP into the first viewport.

### 2. Founder Story Section ("I was the GM. These were my spreadsheets.")

**Is it doing the job?** Yes — this is one of the strongest sections on the page. The headline "I was the GM. These were my spreadsheets." is perfect identity-based persuasion (Cialdini's *Unity* principle — "I am one of you"). The narrative walks through real operational pain: "a 54-member-report would often take me the better part of a day," club-specific language ("F&B committee," "board presentation"), and the origin story of building the tool.

**#1 Fix:** Add a headshot of the founder immediately above or beside the "I was the GM" headline. Bonus: add a short "Former GM at [Named Club]" caption. Named club = specific proof > generic claim.

### 4. Metrics Section ("Intelligence in action.")

**Page Job:** Provide quantified proof that the platform delivers real ROI.

**Is it doing the job?** This is the highest-impact section on the page and it's doing heavy lifting. Four stat cards:
- **6 days** — Average time to first actionable insight for new members
- **91%** — "Of clubs with retention data see improvement" with "Swoop Intelligence"
- **$312** — Average incremental revenue per member through intelligence
- **$1.38M** — Largest identified revenue recovery opportunity

These are strong, specific, and outcome-oriented. They directly speak to the GM's language (revenue, retention, speed).

**But:** None of these metrics are attributed. Who achieved these? Over what time period? At what size club? Without attribution, these feel like marketing claims rather than proof points. There's small gray text beneath each number that I can partially read — it includes some context like "At 1st Tee — CT Flagship club" under the $1.38M metric — but the attribution text is too small and too low-contrast to register.

**#1 Fix:** Make the attribution text larger, bolder, and include the club name prominently. Change from gray subtext to a clear "— [Club Name], [Timeframe]" attribution directly under each number in legible contrast. This single change transforms marketing claims into social proof (Cialdini's *Social Proof* + *Authority* principles).

### 5. Founding Partner Program Section

**Page Job:** Convert interested GMs into applicants for the early-access program.

**Is it doing the job?** This is the primary conversion mechanism on the page and it's underperforming. The section reads:

> "Join the Founding Partner Program."
> "We're inviting 20 clubs to help shape the future of private club intelligence, and giving early adopters real advantages to do so."

Then three benefit columns: **"Hands-on Onboarding"** | **"Shape the Roadmap"** | **"Locked-in Pricing"**

The CTA button: **"Complete a founding partner application"** (orange button)

**Problems:**
1. **"Complete a founding partner application"** — the word "application" implies judgment/rejection. It triggers loss aversion in the wrong direction ("what if I get rejected?") rather than the right direction ("only 20 spots"). Reframe as "Apply for Your Spot" or "Reserve Your Club's Spot."
2. **The three benefits are labels, not specifics.** "Locked-in Pricing" at what price? "Hands-on Onboarding" for how long? "Shape the Roadmap" how — a quarterly call, a Slack channel? Ambiguity = friction.
3. **The "20 clubs" scarcity signal is buried in paragraph text.** This should be a visual callout — a countdown, a progress bar ("7 of 20 spots claimed"), or at minimum bolded and enlarged. Scarcity only works when it's viscerally felt, not intellectually parsed.
4. **No risk reversal.** There's no mention of what happens if you don't like it, how long the commitment is, or whether there's a trial period.

**#1 Fix:** Add a visual scarcity indicator ("X of 20 spots remaining") directly above the CTA button, specify at least one concrete benefit ("Pricing locked at founding rate for 24 months"), and change the CTA copy to "Reserve Your Club's Spot →".

### Additional Observations:

- **No sticky header CTA:** This page is extremely long (7-8 full viewports). Once a visitor scrolls past the hero, there is no persistent CTA visible. A sticky nav with a "Book a Walkthrough" button would capture conversion intent at any scroll depth. This is likely leaving 10-20% of interested visitors without an easy action path.

- **No exit-intent or scroll-depth trigger:** Given the page length and the Founding Partner scarcity angle, a scroll-depth trigger (at 60%+ scroll) showing "Only X founding spots left — want to learn more?" would be a high-value addition.

- **"Book a walkthrough" vs. "Complete a founding partner application"** — these two CTAs serve different commitment levels but aren't clearly differentiated. A visitor doesn't know which one to click. Clarify the distinction: "Not sure yet? Book a 15-min walkthrough" vs. "Ready to join? Apply for a founding spot."

- **The "I was the GM" section is gold but buried.** It's below the fold and requires scrolling past the hero. Consider testing a version where the founder story IS the hero — founder-led SaaS in a niche market (private clubs) benefits enormously from leading with identity and credibility.
